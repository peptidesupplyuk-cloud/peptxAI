Overview

Natural peptide hormone that promotes bonding, reduces stress/anxiety, and enhances emotional connection and social behaviour.

How it works

Oxytocin is a peptide hormone that acts primarily in the brain, binding to oxytocin receptors which are G-protein coupled receptors. This binding triggers a cascade of intracellular events that influence social bonding, stress reduction, and emotional regulation. It modulates neurotransmitter release, particularly in the amygdala and other limbic structures, which are key areas for emotion and social behavior.

What the research describes

  • Enhances social bonding and trust
  • Reduces stress and anxiety levels
  • Facilitates emotional recognition and empathy
  • Promotes maternal behaviors
  • May improve symptoms of autism spectrum disorders

Health optimisation

Oxytocin may support mental health by reducing stress and enhancing social interactions, contributing to overall well-being.

Evidence summary

There are several human trials that have investigated oxytocin's effects on social behavior and stress reduction. These studies generally support its role in enhancing social interactions and reducing anxiety, though results can be variable. More research is needed to fully understand its therapeutic potential and long-term effects.

Evidence grade B, Emerging.

Oxytocin is typically administered intranasally for behavioral effects. Dosing can vary, but common regimens involve 24-48 IU per day, divided into two doses. Cycling is not generally required, but long-term effects are not well-studied.
